# Demystifying CIDR Notation, MAC Addresses, and DNS

## What I learn?
Imagine you’ve invited friends, family, and neighbors from all over. To ensure the event runs smoothly, you meticulously plan a seating chart, assign table numbers, and provide clear directions to your house.
Now, think of networking as hosting this party — but on a massive scale. Instead of guests, there are devices like phones, laptops, and servers, all needing specific instructions on how to find each other, where to go, and how to interact. And just like your party wouldn’t work without a seating chart or directions, networking wouldn’t function without some key components: CIDR Notation, MAC Addresses, and DNS.

- CIDR Notation (Classless Inter-Domain Routing): Think of it as your seating plan for the devices in your “network party.” It decides how many seats (IP addresses) are available and how they are grouped.
- MAC Address: This is like your guests’ unique ID cards, ensuring each person (device) has a distinct identity.
- DNS (Domain Name System): This acts as the GPS or the address book, translating user-friendly names like “www.partytime.com" into the exact location (IP address) where the party is happening.
